The Uncertainty Principle

How sure are you?

Uncertainty principle banner caption.

Way down at the quantum scale, one thing you can be absolutely sure of is uncertainty. Indeed, one of the bedrock tenets of quantum science is the famous and frequently misinterpreted Uncertainty Principle, formulated by Werner Heisenberg in the 1920s.

No matter what you may have heard, the principle does not say that by observing a quantum event, we inevitably influence the outcome.⊕ ⊕ That's often true, but entirely irrelevant. Nor does it say that we can’t make any quantum measurements with certainty.

What it does say is that, for certain pairs of complementary properties—such as the position and momentum of a quantum object—the more precisely we measure one, the less precisely we can pin down the other. That’s not a statement about some flaw or limit to our ability to observe things. Rather, it describes a fundamental trade-off built into nature.

As Heisenberg put it:

“One can never know with perfect accuracy both of those two important factors which determine the movement of one of the smallest particles—its position and its velocity. It is impossible to determine accurately both the position and the direction and speed of a particle at the same instant.”

The Uncertainty Principle is also responsible for a phenomenon that would seem totally outlandish if it wasn’t physically real: “virtual” particles that pop in and out of existence in empty space. This happens because time and energy are complementary properties, too, meaning that over short time intervals, quantum physics doesn’t prohibit big energy fluctuations. One way of thinking about those fluctuations is tiny particles and their partner antiparticles popping out from nothing and recombining in an instant. You’ll never catch them in an experiment, but their effects are real.

Paradoxically, the uncertainty principle can be used to actually increase the certainty in a measurement if you’re only interested in one of two complementary variables. The principle says that multiplying the uncertainty in one property times the uncertainty in the other can never exceed a specific fixed value. So, you can get greater accuracy in measuring one property if you’re willing to put up with or disregard uncertainty in the complementary property.

Position and velocity are complementary properties. By dragging the blue circle, representing the shared uncertainty, you can "squeeze" uncertainty from one property into another.

This process is called “squeezing.” In the case of light, amplitude (brightness) and phase (position of wave peaks relative to a given point) are complementary properties. By squeezing light, you can get greatly improved accuracy in measuring the amplitude at the expense of decreasing the information about phase.
