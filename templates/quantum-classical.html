Quantum vs. Classical

What makes the quantum world different?

Size is one way to distinguish the quantum world from the classical world, although it's not a perfect separation.

Everyday objects basically obey the laws of physics discovered by Isaac Newton and others over the past four centuries. Your car keys (usually) stay right where you put them an hour ago and don’t suddenly appear in the refrigerator. You can plan an outfit the night before because your clothes don’t change color in the closet. A well-pitched baseball always arrives at the catcher’s mitt and not in the parking lot.

These everyday experiences are the result of classical physics—the laws and equations that were around before quantum physics. The world of classical physics is predictable. If you know the initial location and speed of an object, as well as the forces that act on it, you can predict its motion with perfect certainty. That’s how NASA can place a spacecraft at a precise location a billion kilometers away.

In the quantum world—the world that emerges at the scale of atoms or smaller⊕ ⊕ Or when things get really, really cold.—things are utterly different. Not spooky or mysterious or incomprehensible. Just way different.

For one thing, properties of quantum objects can’t take on any old value. Someone walking down a ramp can stop anywhere along the way, but quantum properties are restricted to certain values—more like a staircase than a ramp. Something can be on step 2, 3, 4 or whatever. But not step 2.67 or 4.23. It’s just the way nature seems to operate at the smallest scales. Each of these increments is a "quantum⊕ ⊕ Plural: quanta.,” from the Latin word for “how much”. Thus, quantum physics.

Some quantum effects are easier to understand than others. For example, each element in the periodic table has its own unique luminous fingerprint called a spectrum. That’s because each has its own distinct arrangement of electrons with certain specific energies—a staircase of energy levels determined by quantum physics.

When one of the electrons gains exactly the right amount of energy—no more, no less—it can jump⊕ ⊕ When electrons change levels, they do so instantaneously in a “quantum leap.” to a higher energy level. Eventually it falls back down, and it sheds that energy as a quantum of light—also known as a photon. The color of the light depends on the energy difference between the two levels, and so is unique to every element.

Many more properties, including aspects of magnetism, are quantized on the atomic scale. But quantization does not mean that an object’s properties, as we observe them, are predetermined. In fact, isolated quantum objects or systems do not have any particular properties until they are measured, a feature you can learn more about in the entry on quantum measurement. Quantum physics provides a way to calculate the probability of any given measurement outcome, though, even if it doesn’t provide the perfect predictability of classical physics.

The yellow color of a sodium vapor lamp is a direct result of the spectrum of sodium atoms.

Finally, in the quantum world objects can behave like both particles and waves, depending on the situation. Although massless photons appear like waves, and can be reflected, refracted and so forth, they also act like particles in certain experiments. For example, when a photon with the right energy strikes some materials, it can liberate an electron from its usual cramped position. This interaction, called the photoelectric effect, earned Einstein a Nobel prize and serves as the basis for every photovoltaic cell that makes electricity out of light from the sun.

Ordinary matter like electrons also have a fickle personality. As particles, they are used in lithography, welding, chemical processing, medicine and many more applications. But when a beam of electrons passes through a barrier with two parallel slits and then on to a detector, the electrons behave like waves, forming interference patterns that you can learn more about in the entry on quantum superposition.
