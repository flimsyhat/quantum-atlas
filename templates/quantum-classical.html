Quantum vs. Classical

What makes the quantum world different?

Size is one way to distinguish the quantum world from the classical world, although it's not a perfect separation.

Everyday objects more or less obey the laws of physics discovered by Isaac Newton and other luminaries of the Enlightenment. You can (usually) find your car keys because they stay right where you put them an hour ago. They don’t suddenly appear in the refrigerator. You can decide how to dress because your clothes don’t change color in the closet. A well-pitched baseball always arrives at the catcher’s mitt and not in the parking lot.

These everyday experiences are the result of classical physics—the laws and equations that were around before quantum mechanics. The world of classical physics is predictable. If you know the initial position and speed of an object, as well as the forces that act on it, you can predict its motion with perfect certainty. That’s how NASA can place a spacecraft at a precise location a billion kilometers away.

But in the quantum world—at the scale of atoms or smaller—things are utterly different. Not weird or mysterious or incomprehensible. Just way different.

For one thing, properties of quantum objects can’t take on any old value. Someone walking down a ramp can stop anywhere along the way, but many quantum properties are restricted to certain allowed values—more like items placed on a staircase than on a ramp. Something can be on step 2, 3, 4 or whatever. But not step 2.67 or 4.23. It’s the way nature seems to operate at the smallest scales.

Each increment is a “quantum,” from the Latin word for “how much” (plural: quanta). [CC1] Some quantum effects defy intuition, but others are easier to understand. For example, each element in the periodic table has its own unique luminous fingerprint, which is also called a spectrum. That’s because each has its own distinct arrangement of electrons with certain specific energies.

When one electron gains exactly the right amount of energy—no more, no less—it can jump to a higher energy level. Eventually it falls back down, and it sheds that energy as a quantum of light—also known as a photon. The color of the light depends on the energy difference between the two levels, and so is unique to every element. (When electrons change levels, they do so instantaneously in a “quantum leap.”)

The yellow color of a sodium vapor lamp is a direct result of the spectrum of sodium atoms.

Many more properties, including aspects of magnetism, are quantized on the atomic scale. But that does not mean that an object’s properties have fixed values. In fact, isolated quantum objects or systems do not have any particular properties until they are measured, a feature you can learn more about in the entry on quantum measurement. Quantum physics provides a way to calculate the probability of any given measurement outcome, though, even if it doesn’t provide the perfect predictability of classical physics.

Finally, in the quantum world objects can behave like both particles and waves, depending on the situation. Although massless photons behave like waves, and can be reflected, refracted and so forth, they also behave like particles in certain experiments. For example, when a photon with the right energy strikes some materials, it can liberate an electron from its usual cramped position. This interaction, called the photoelectric effect, got Einstein a Nobel prize and serves as the basis for every photovoltaic cell that makes electricity out of light from the sun.

Ordinary matter like electrons also have a fickle personality. As particles, they are used in lithography, welding, chemical processing, medicine and many more applications. But when a beam of electrons passes through a barrier with two parallel slits and then on to a detector, the electrons behave like waves, forming interference patterns on the detector.
