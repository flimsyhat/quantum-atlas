Quantum Superposition

A fundamental—and familiar—feature of quantum physics.

Ripples in a pond or a pool capture one of the most essential ideas in quantum physics.

Every time you strum a guitar and hear the tones from each string harmonize into a rich chord, you’re experiencing the teamwork of waves. When two waves bump into each other, they tend to join forces. It’s something that also happens on the surface of a pond after tossing in a handful of pebbles—the ripples bump into each other and interfere on their journey toward the shore.

Both water waves in a pond and sound waves from a guitar capture a generic feature: Overlapping waves combine, blending their undulations by addition. The resulting wave is just a point-by-point sum of individual waves that yields a single combined whole called a superposition.

When you click or tap to create a wave, it travels outward from a central point. When two waves overlap, they interfere and yield brighter or darker spots than any single wave.

The quantum world has its own version of this pairing in which two or more quantum waves—often representing very different physical situations—merge into a single quantum whole. Sometimes it seems incomprehensibly weird, like plopping an apple next to an orange and calling the pair a banana, but it’s very real.

Whereas ordinary waves represent the movements of water or air molecules, quantum waves are different--They represent the likelihood of something happening. And while water waves alternate between crests and troughs, quantum waves swing between higher and lower probabilities for various quantum properties.

One of those properties might be energy. For instance, an electron bound to an atom can live at particular energy levels. In a superposition, it may have a chance of having one of two energies—a common situation in quantum physics—so that the two waves representing those two energies get added together.

An electron in an atom gets smeared out into a cloud of possibilities known as an orbital.

It’s common to describe this by saying that the electron has two different energies at the same time. But that’s like saying the ripples on a pond are in many places at once; it’s true, but it isn’t all that helpful to talk about waves in those terms. Waves, by nature, are extended things, and it’s the properties of the whole wave—the frequency of its oscillations and the speed at which it travels, among others—that matter.

Perhaps surprisingly, the effects of quantum superposition can be seen at real-world scales, much bigger than the submicroscopic realm of electrons and atoms. In one experiment, a beam of electrons was fired at a barrier containing two narrow slits, past which sat a sensitive detector that could register where an electron struck—kind of like a camera. If electrons were behaving like tiny billiard balls, you would expect the detector to see a pattern with two clusters of arrivals, one directly behind each slit. Instead, the detector saw an interference pattern, as though individual electrons were interfering with themselves.

In the double-slit experiment, individual electrons hit a screen and build up an interference pattern. If the electrons were merely particles, there would be no such pattern.

It’s an experiment that challenges intuition, but quantum physics easily explains what’s going on. The two slits expose the wave-like behavior of single electrons by channeling them into a quantum superposition of “went through the left slit” and “went through the right slit.” Adding up the wave patterns generated by the two paths yields the pattern of alternating bands that the detector actually records.

So the existence of quantum superpositions isn’t really up for debate. Still, some of the greatest minds in science have struggled with how to interpret quantum superposition as well as other features of quantum physics, and that struggle is ongoing. There are now almost as many interpretations of quantum physics as there are quantum physicists, but they all agree that superposition is a necessary—and real—feature of the theory.
